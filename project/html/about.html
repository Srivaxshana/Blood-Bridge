Benefits

Donating blood has benefits for both your body and mind. Research suggests that helping others can:

  • Saves Lives
  • Keeps Your Heart Healthy
  • Helps Your Body Make New Blood
  • Lowers Cancer Risk
  • Free Health Check-Up
  • Makes You Feel Happy
  • Supports Your Community

Free health checkup

Blood donation includes a free health check-up, helping donors monitor their health and detect potential issues early.

  • Detects Health Issues Early – Blood donation includes tests that can identify conditions like anemia, infections, or irregular blood pressure.
  • Monitors Blood Pressure & Pulse – Donors get their blood pressure, pulse rate, and hemoglobin levels checked before donating.
  • Checks for Infections – Blood is tested for diseases like hepatitis, HIV, and syphilis, ensuring safety for both donors and recipients.
  • Encourages Regular Health Monitoring – Frequent donors get regular health check-ups, helping them stay aware of their health status.
  • Provides a Basic Health Report – Donors are informed if any abnormalities are found, allowing them to seek medical advice early.
  • Boosts Awareness of Overall Health – Encourages people to maintain a healthy lifestyle to be eligible for future donations.
  • Completely Free of Cost – Donors receive these health benefits without any expense.

Side effects of donating blood

Blood donation is generally safe, but some donors may experience mild side effects that usually go away quickly.
Call the blood donation center if:

  • Temporary drop in blood pressure may cause mild dizziness.
  • Some donors feel tired after donation but recover with rest and hydration.
  • A small bruise may appear at the needle site due to blood vessel irritation.
  • Some may experience mild discomfort in the arm.
  • Feeling slightly nauseous is possible, especially for first-time donors.
  • Rare cases may experience temporary low blood pressure.
  • Frequent donors should maintain a healthy iron-rich diet.

During the donation

During the donation, the process is quick and simple, ensuring your comfort while collecting the blood needed to help others.

For a whole blood donation procedure:

  1. Registration – The donor provides personal details and completes a health questionnaire.
  2. Health Check-Up – A quick check of blood pressure, pulse, temperature, and hemoglobin levels is done to ensure the donor is fit to donate.
  3. Preparation – The donor is seated in a comfortable position, and the arm is cleaned with an antiseptic.
  4. Blood Collection – A sterile needle is inserted into a vein, and about 350-450 ml of blood is collected in a bag. The process takes 8-10 minutes.
  5. Relaxation – After donation, the needle is removed, and a bandage is applied to the site. The donor rests for a few minutes.
  6. Refreshments – Donors are given fluids and light snacks to help regain energy.
  7. Recovery & Guidance – Donors are advised to avoid heavy lifting or strenuous activities for a few hours.

What to know before you donate

Before donating blood, it's important to be aware of certain guidelines and prepare properly to ensure a safe and successful donation.

  • Eligibility Requirements – You must be at least 17 years old (16 with parental consent), weigh at least 110 lbs (50 kg), and be in good health.
  • No Recent Illness – Make sure you’re feeling well and haven’t had a cold, flu, or other illness in the past 24 hours.
  • Hydrate – Drink plenty of water before donating to help your body handle the donation better.
  • Eat Well – Have a light meal or snack before donating. Avoid fatty foods, as they can affect the quality of the blood.
  • Avoid Alcohol and Caffeine – Refrain from drinking alcohol or caffeinated drinks before donating, as they can dehydrate you.
  • Bring ID – Be ready to show identification when registering to donate.
  • Rest Before Donation – Make sure you’ve had enough rest (at least 5-6 hours of sleep the night before).
  • Medications – Inform the blood donation center about any medications you're taking, as some may affect your eligibility.
  • Know Your Blood Type – If you’re unsure of your blood type, it’s a good idea to find out before donating.
  • Donation Frequency – Whole blood can be donated once every 56 days, while platelet donations can be made every 7 days, up to 24 times per year.
